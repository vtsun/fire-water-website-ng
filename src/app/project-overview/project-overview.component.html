Project Background

In recent years, wildfires have increased and spread rapidly, and have become more difficult to control, especially in the western US. The cumulative impacts of wildland fire on watersheds have not been fully understood, especially given climate-induced increases in frequency and severity of fire. The examination of water quality in areas affected by wildfires is still lacking, putting millions of Americans at risk. It is of critical importance to investigate the potential impact of climate changes on wildfire and seek management options.

Prescribed fires are widely used to confront the wildfire crises and maintain the health of US forests. Previous studies have suggested wildland fires could elevate sediment and nutrient concentration threatening water supplies. However, how prescribed fires will impact water quality and quantity have not been fully understood especially in a changing climate. Examination of water quality in the areas affected by prescribed fires is still lacking, putting millions of Americans at risk. It is of critical importance to understand the state of knowledge, identify research gaps and explore potential modeling approached of the benefits and potential impacts of prescribed fires on water quality in the Eastern US.

Our research projects assess cumulative effects of wildfire on watershed water quantity and quality using advanced tools that include hydrological models and fire disturbance models. Using a integrated modeling system, we will investigate the potential impacts of changing climate and wildfire regimes on watershed functions that are critical to clean water supply in the U.S.

Specifically, the project aims to:

  1. Improve existing forest hydrological models and Forests to Faucets (F2F) decision support tools to target catchments for restoration activities to improve water quality, and:
  2. Synthesize the state of knowledge, identify research gaps, and explore potential modeling approaches of the benefits and potential impacts of prescribed fires on water quality in the East.

The USDA Forest Service has developed a 10-year plan to deal with wildland fire and this project will develop the necessary tools and science for implement the plan. At the same time, research outcomes (tools and trained personals) are useful for training future scientists for protecting drinking water supply in many impacted watersheds in eastern and western forests.

Project Timeline and Tasks

This project will be developed in two phases.

The first phase is from 4/1/2023‒ 9/30/2023 and the second phase starts from 10/1/2023– 12/31/2024.

Task 1 (phase 1, 4/1/2023‒12/31/2023): database and model revisions

Scientists at USFS will work together with collaborators on developing an integrated database and a simulation tool for assessing cumulative effects of wildfire on watersheds by coupling hydrologic models with a fire disturbance model. The fire disturbance model can simulate burn severity patterns and creates new soil and land cover types characterized by the burn severity. Modeled burn severity will be compared to fire severity maps developed from remote sensing.

Task 2 (phase 2, 1/1/2024‒12/31/2024):

Using WaSSI-Fire model or DHSVM-Fire, USFS and collaborators will investigate the potential impacts of changing climate and wildfire regimes on watersheds, including effects on disturbance and stress interactions, streamflow, and post-fire ecological processes in Region 9 and 10 of EPA and Region 8 of FS.

Task 3 (phase 2, 1/1/2025‒9/30/2025):

In consultation with USFS, collaborators will prepare the datasets and maps resulting from the simulations and analyses that will be made available to FS. USFS and Duke will prepare a manuscript describing the context, datasets, modeling approach, key results, and conclusions of this research.

Task 4 (phase 2, 10/1/2025‒3/31/2026):

Explore potential effects of fire management practices could reduce fire impacts to water quantity and quality through scenarios development and analysis using the validated DHSVM-Fire and WaSSI-Fire. The scenarios include different management practices during three distinct time periods, e.g., no recovery, initial recovery and fully recovered after burned. The fire management options include prescribed burning, forest thinning/timber harvesting, or mechanical treatment.

Literature Review

Forest lands provide the best water quality among all land uses (Sun et al., 2004; Caldwell et al., 2023). The eastern U.S. is heavily covered by forests, managed and unmanaged, providing a majority of the surface water supply for domestic and industrial uses, agricultural irrigation and benefiting millions of people and the local economies (Liu et al., 2022). However, exacerbated by climate change, forest disturbances induced by drought, flood, hurricanes, insects, and diseases, and wildland fires negatively affect the hydrologic and biogeochemical cycles at multiple scales and elevate pollutant loading to downstream watersheds (Sun and Lockaby, 2012; Sun et al., 2013). Forest restoration is becoming more important to respond these environmental changes (Stanturf and Mansourian, 2020). For example, use of prescribed fire, a management strategy intended to build forest ecosystem resilience to wildfire and climate change, has been projected to increase (Liu et al. 2014; Vose et al. 2021; Greenberg and Collins, 2021).

Irrespective of disturbances, several site-specific factors can affect surface water quality, including management activities, land use history (legacy effects), land cover, soil, topography, rainfall, flow patterns, and disturbance characteristics (Hallema et al., 2018; Caldwell et al., 2023). Landscape scale restoration activities such as prescribed fires, reforestation, tree species conversion, and wetland rehabilitation can improve water quantity and quality (Smith et al., 2021; Paul et al., 2022). For example, prescribed fires that are widely used in longleaf pine restoration and loblolly pine conversions are likely to reduce plant competition for soil moisture during drought, and thus enhance ecosystem resilience and water availability to aquatic ecosystems (Brantley et al., 2017). Many studies in the southern U.S. suggest prescribed fires that normally have low severity generally do not cause concerns of water quality (Sun et al., 2004) and fire generally has less effect on sediment loading in the Southern United States than in the Western United States (Crumbley et al., 2007). Increased soil erosion following fires is frequently associated with forest floor disturbances caused by mechanical site preparation during fire controlling activities (Van Lear, 1985). Similarly, operationally disturbed sites and especially skid trails have been found to be more susceptible to erosion following fires. Most fire research in the Southern Appalachians has involved fires of low-to-moderate intensity (Swift and others 1993). The most important water quality improvement goal in forest restorations is reducing suspended sediment although other variables such as nutrients, organic carbon, and mercury are alse of concern (Chou et al., 2021). Severe wildland fires could elevate sediment concentration in the Appalachians (Caldwell et al., 2020) threatening water supplies (Pau et al., 2022; Bladon et al., 2014).

Overall, the effectiveness of forest restoration decisions is constrained by both natural and socioeconomic factors (Stanturf and Mansourian, 2020; Jones et al., 2022). Quantifying benefits of restoration activities is challenging, thus more integrated studies are needed. It is not practical to conduct restoration activities everywhere for improving water quality and aquatic habitat due to cost constraints. Federal and non-Federal land managers need advanced tools that can be used to support ecosystem restoration decision making (e.g., Watershed Restoration Action Plan) so that restoration resources can target the most critical water quality hotspots to insure continued provisioning of water-related ecosystem services.

Section 40804(b)10 of the Forestry Provisions in the BIL: Landscape scale restoration to restore water quality and fish passage provides direction and funding for ecosystem restoration but there is a need to identify the most fruitful areas for restoration. Due to the complexity of hydrological and biogeochemical responses to landscape restoration activities under different climatic and socioeconomic conditions, advanced decision support tools are needed for restoration planning purposes (Mashal et al., 2023; Jones et al. 2022). Recent literature review suggests existing hydrological and water quality modeling tools are promising for quantifying effects of forest restoration (Qi et al., 2022), but have challenges to apply to watersheds affects by forest fires and restoration due to the lack of empirical data (Crumbley et al., 2007; Ebel et al., 2023).
